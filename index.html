    A geographic information system (GIS) is a system that creates, manages, analyzes, and maps all types of data. GIS connects data to a map, integrating location data (where things are) with all types of descriptive information (what things are like there). This provides a foundation for mapping and analysis that is used in science and almost every industry. GIS helps users understand patterns, relationships, and geographic context. The benefits include improved communication and efficiency as well as better management and decision making. ~ESRI

    Academic Writing Sample

    Trailblazing Toronto

     

    The idea of evolution is that eventually, outliers can, and will, either tweak or kill the status quo. Within the article provided, Jennifer Keesmaat is portrayed positively as a radical celebrity evolutionist, in the planning sense. She is the ‘irrepressibly idealistic chief planner’ that this article surrounds. 

    She is introduced as a leading proponent of a seemingly staggering proposal to remove the arterial highway governing the main mode of transport within Toronto. This may at first feel overwhelming, as all those familiar with Toronto identify the Gardiner Express highway infrastructure as integral. Well, almost everyone. This grand appeal is presented as a gesture of repertoire that all great revolutions require. The appeal is presented as a long overdue moment of climax for Keesmaat and is detailed with great depth. From descriptions of the setting, the tacit of conversation, to the dress code is described as Keesmaat’s “I am Spartacus” moment. The significance of this introduction is the contest presented to Toronto mayor John Tory and his plan, representative of all political nuance and dance. 

    The situation described, where the anagnorisis of openly presenting an idea as grand as removing the Gardiner Express way, serves to politically illustrate the theme of current climate for change within planning. This article wholly serves as a query of intent for struggle or revolution, in the marketplace of ideas, where a dominant party decides the future of many. On this stage, the revolutionist is played by Jennifer Keesmaat, as the catalyst for communal change.

    The main action of this article draws my concern towards three points of interest surrounding the prompt for this piece of writing. Two of these instances surround an involvement where Toronto mayor, John Tory, meets with Keesmaat to tell her to ‘zip it’, as in, to stop tweeting and retweeting her ideas and any copious support for them. This, in light of the instructions for this topic of discussion, show the ill-wit of politics, where disturbance to those of power, or to the status quo, may translate to adversity.

    Regarding the prompt: 

    “The politician must deal with two sets of interests; one set comprises the views held by the public (including developers) and the other comprises the advice given by the community's planners and other technical advisors.”

    Primarily, towards Tory, or those who intend to appease the status quo, it is undemocratic to silence knowledge/expertise, opinions/positions. Also, as mentioned in the prompt, those within the community are also worthy of consultation. This is relative for the usage of twitter by Keesmaat, which was quite democratic and ironically involving. 

    The remainder of the article is ultimately falling action, providing mostly character development and background. A universal tale is developed though, through this manner, which will be described as the conclusion. A key part to the story that this article tells regards the last point of consensus. Concerning that one bit of the prompt above: “(including developers)”,  a side or sub story is told. In this trial-like sub-story showdown, a development that would “create a trio of 8o-plus-storey Jenga towers” was modified by Keesmaat to a friendlier design of two buildings that were not described as clumsy behemoths. Instead, these resulting collaborative buildings were praised and proclaimed to be the much better route, and thus, subsequent development.

    To conclude with the answer to the prompt question: 

    “Is the Planner's responsibility to her employer (City Council)?”

    No, because democracy dictates service first to citizens, from governance. The tale of radical evolution has many faces and most of them include adversity. This theme will certainly surface numerous times en route to a more sustainable future, where there will be another Gardiner Express way, more John Tory’s, and numerous opportunities spearheaded by valiant hardworking hero’s such as Toronto’s very own, Jennifer Keesmaat. 

    The overarching thematic excitement about this article is that we, as in those of any relation to Toronto, are granted a prospect to set precedent. A chance to be beheld as a worldly exemplar, where Toronto trailblazes a new territory of courageous development, taking the torch, changing the norm. The article ends with a dare to “believe that anything can happen if you want it badly enough”.
